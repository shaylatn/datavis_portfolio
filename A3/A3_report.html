Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I wanted to see a side by side comparison of the age proportions
  • It is easy to compare slice sizes and subsets of age groups between the years
  • Important points such as 25% or 50% are easily identifiable
  • Outer slices may "appear" larger than inner slices due to having a larger radius which could mislead population size
  • This allows us to see proportions really well but I would love to also see the counts.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • This better depicts overall change in population as well as age group changes from 1900 to 2000
  • It maintains some of the proportionality benefits. You can see what proportion of the population a certain age group takes up.
  • It is more difficult to compare proportions across the years since the heights of the bars are different.
  • It provides a visualization of cumulative counts but its difficult to determine the individual counts of each age group.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • It shows the count of each age group for each year.
  • You can see which groups had the biggest change.
  • I wish I added some line between the points for each year so we could visually compare the sizes of the lines across age groups.
  • The graph is very simple and doesn't show much more than the datasheet tells us.

Reflection

Overall, each sketch has its pros and cons. I made each successive sketch after finding something wrong with the current iteration. However, with adding a new feature means I would sacrifice a benefit of the previous sketch. I believe they're all good for different use cases. If you didn't care about the actual size of the population and more just the age distribution then I would use the double pie chart as the stacked nature of it allows for very easy comparisons and estimations. However, if the differences in population is important to you then I would use the stacked bar chart as opposed to the scatter plot. This is because the information in the scatterplot can be found in the stack bars with a little bit of math (subtracting the bottom boundary from the top). It visualizes population differences while also giving you an idea of what the age distribution of a given year looks like.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

For my design process, I wanted to emphasize proportions over the actual population count. We know that population has increased overtime but this can happen in many ways. The population can increase proportionally, with the same fraction of people landing in each bucket or we could see a trend that over the 100 years people tend to live shorter or longer and in this case, we can see that they got older.

I decided to use a pie chart as opposed to the stacked bar chart because going around the circle, we know exactly how much is one whole, as opposed to the bar chart where it can be various heights which makes comparing proportion slices more difficult. With the pie chart, if the slice lines line up then you know those are equal proportionally. Additionally, I ordered the slices in increasing age order starting at the positive y axis and going around. This is so we can subset adjacent age groups so we can see if the population is generally young or old without having to add up disconnected slices. Since both pie charts go in the same order, we can comapre age groups between the two years.

Reflection

I believe my first sketch best represents my question of "how did the age distribution differ from 1900 to 2000" pretty well and allows a reader to focus on the proportions which is why I chose to create it.
