Proposition: The standard of living has increased for everyone.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I cut off the data to begin at 1980. This is because prior to 1980, the chart did not follow the trend as strongly, so to create the largest visual decrease, I cut the range. I would give this a score of 1 since it is pretty representative of the data with minimal filtering.
  • I used data from all countries. This makes it more representative of the total world population and allows readers to make general trends which gives it s core of 2.
  • Looking at it now, I wish I added some label data points to emphasize high and low points in the line graph. Since the trend is already pretty clear, this would only help to emphasize it, not really adding any deceptiveness and thus grant a score of 1.5.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I filtered out several countries in order to get this trend in order to show that world population data cannot generalize to individual countries. I would give this a score of -1 since I purposefully filtered that data to only include specific countries.
  • In the title of the graph, I don't specify the population which again allows me to cherry pick countries that supports the claim. This scores -.5 since it acts as an accomplice to the first design choice.
  • Additionally, I did not color code the points as to not show what countries they are from/represent. Once again, this just furthers the decpetion that the data is not representative of the whole population. Score: -1
  • Ideally, I wanted to have some type of histogram to show the percentage or income buckets that the population fell in in order to show that most of the wealth is only handled by a few which would negate the fact that overall standard of living is increasing, but I was not able to find the right measures and dimensions to do so.

Final Reflection

Overall, I felt this was extremely difficult for me to find evidence for oppsing sides of an opposition. I wanted to utilize Simpson's Paradox and find trends that didn't hold when the population was divided, but due to the large datasets, it was very difficult to find these subpopulations. A simple tactic that I noticed was that if one part of the timeline didnt support your claim, you could (relatively easily) just cut that time out of your data.

With regards to my visualizations, I found that most of the data was able to support my proposition with minimal changes. Thus my 'for' visualization is a pretty straight forward representation of the data. However, becasue of this, it was extremely difficult to make my against visualization. I tried to use different measures that could still imply standrad of living but most followed the same trends. Then I tried excluding a few of the top 1st world countries but still couldn't make much progress. The deception techniques that I used in my second visualization can be misleading, but they can also be beneficial depending on the argument that is made. It is deceptive if one wants to make a generalization on the whole world population. However, a part of the reason I used it was to indicate that generalizations on the world are not necessarily applicable on a country basis, and thus this second visual shows that for the countries included, most of the wealth is held by a small population.

I believe that ethical design visualization doesn't pick and choose what data they want to include. It's okay to maybe exclude a specific population but not to cheery pick data points that don't support the claim which can lead to lots of misinformation. I also believe its very important to identify the population you want to make conclusions about since oftentimes subpopulation data cannot be generalized to a larger overall population and vice versa.
